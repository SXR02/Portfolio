BrumGo Project

Through my experience during School of Code, I was able to develop UX/UI foundational knowledge which I applied to real-life scenarios

Problem

The problem to solve for the group, was to encourage better tourism within the city, to give better visisbility of Birmingham heritage and culture. There was currently no specfic app that provided information in personalise experience. The desire outcome, tourists were are able to make the most of their visit, based on their location.

Solution

A geo-fenced app which provides facts, places of interest, restaurants, entertainment, hidden gems, and tourist information based on their current location. This would be information that would not be obtained from competitors like e.g. google maps, because the information would be regularly updated, and unique information specfic to Birmingham.

Approach

To create a solution, it was important to build a minimum viable product, this included, planning what must, should and could be included within the app. From a design perspective, this would be important to understand the content I would have to design around.

Requirements

To keep the MVP simple and achievable, there were three key aspects as a group we agreed would achieve the set goal.

  • Login Page
  • Map page
  • Pop-up functionality, to indicate points of interest i.e. image, text and or AR feature

My Objective

To help the team, materialise the solution to the problem we were attempting to solve.

User Persona

The user persona was a useful method in helping creating the potential users that would utilise the app. The key here, was not to create a list of all users, but rather based on a survey and the data obtained, would help identify common themes such as lifestyle, and key motivation as to why users would use the app. This would help identify whether we had captured the right requirements for the app.

Wireframing process

From the initial planning, it was important to talk through and sketch out the user flow and get understand the potential functionality of the app itself. I was able to connect the potential information and the visual design to show the path between the key pages. This helped identify any flaws we wouldn't have otherwise considered as part of initial plannning. The paper based wireframing would prove to be very helpful, as collective would agree what the MVP could look like.

My task was to translate the MVP, into high-fidelity wireframe. The key was to remember the wireframe should be able to satisfy the early consumers and to provide feedback for furture product development. I used Figma, which was the go-to tool used mainly within the course, to draft an initial design of our app. The design tool help me visualise the app to the team. It open up conversation, on whether our early assumptions needed to be challenged and help me take on feedback to improve the design.

I shared the MVP developed in Figma with the team, which helped visualise app. One potential improvement was to enhance the point of interests on the map itself. The screen on the left showed the basic intention to simply display in a list form, as this would satisfy the users. However, one potential improvement from the team was to enhance the points of interests on the map itself. From a design perspective, I now improved the view to focus on the map, with markers included, demonstrated on the right screen. I continued to iterate in this way with small improvements.

Coding

I was coding in react-native, which was mobile version of react. It was first time coding in react-native, and a noticable challenge, was the styling. In React Native you don't have CSS styling, you have JavaScript styling and often this meant I had to style inline rather than what I was used to with CSS Modules.

Project Delivery

I worked in day sprints, making sure I had coded the component for the team to review and provide feedback before it would be pushed to main branch. This helpful as a Zenhub helped organise the work breakdown structure, and commiting the code into Github
